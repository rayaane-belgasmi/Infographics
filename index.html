Demystifying the Jargon DPI/DPT, Purdue & IEC/ISA‑62443 (Series)

Purdue = floors (vertical levels). IEC/ISA‑62443 is a series of security standards here we use the ideas of zones and approved paths (conduits).

Elevator Summary
Purdue = Floors
Stack from physical process (L0) to enterprise (L4); move one floor at a time.
IEC/ISA‑62443 series
A family of security standards. We reference zones & conduits (approved paths) and default‑deny.
DMZ (L3.5)
Security lobby that brokers, checks, and logs crossings between operations ↔ enterprise.
Story: the journey through the Levels
1) Measure (L0)
A sensor captures a value in the physical world.
2) Control (L1)
A controller reacts immediately to keep things safe and on‑spec.
3) Supervise (L2)
An operator screen shows the run and logs to a shared place.
4) Coordinate (L3)
Site services file, store, and organize the data for the team.
5) Cross the Lobby (L3.5 → L4)
Approved data crosses the DMZ to business tools.
Standards & Norms What the Names Mean
ISA‑95 / IEC 62264 (Purdue)
Defines the levels/floors from equipment to enterprise.
IEC/ISA‑62443 series
Security for industrial/operations systems zones, conduits, default‑deny, least privilege, accountability.
Common practice
Use a DMZ (L3.5), a jump‑host for admin access, and documented approved paths (conduits).
Levels What goes here, why it’s here, plain English
L0 · Process Process
What goes here
  • Sensors, valves, motors, scales, field devices
  • Wiring, signal lines, actuators
Why it’s here
  • Keep the physical process working even if computers fail
  • Safety and continuity first
Plain English

The real‑world parts taps, pumps, heaters must work regardless of Wi‑Fi or email.

L1 · Basic Control Control
What goes here
  • PLCs, embedded controllers, dedicated control modules
Why it’s here
  • React in milliseconds to keep the process safe and stable
  • Local logic independent from office networks
Plain English

The cruise‑control of operations reacts instantly without cloud help.

L2 · Supervisory / HMI HMI
What goes here
  • Operator workstations, SCADA/HMI servers, instrument PCs
Why it’s here
  • Provide a single view to monitor and adjust many L0/L1 devices
  • Record runs and operator actions
Plain English

The dashboard one screen to watch many gauges and make safe tweaks.

L3 · Site Operations & Services Services
What goes here
  • Site data stores (file shares, historians)
  • Scheduling, middleware, local apps
Why it’s here
  • Coordinate work across many stations
  • Keep copies of runs and configurations in one place
Plain English

The site’s filing cabinet and planner organizes and stores what happened.

L3.5 · DMZ / Buffer DMZ
What goes here
  • Jump‑host for admin/vendor access (MFA + logging)
  • Proxies, allow‑listed transfer/broker services
  • Patch/update mirrors (where used)
Why it’s here
  • Act as a security lobby between operations and enterprise
  • Check, log, and tightly control anything that crosses
Plain English

Airport border control for data and access everyone shows a passport here.

L4 · Enterprise / Business Enterprise
What goes here
  • Email, ERP, collaboration, analytics platforms
Why it’s here
  • Run the business side without touching real‑time control
  • Consume curated data for reporting and decisions
Plain English

Head office planning and reporting tools kept separate from machines.

IEC/ISA‑62443 Series Concepts we reference
Zones
Group similar‑risk assets behind the same rules.
Conduits
Approved, documented paths between zones.
Default‑deny
Doors stay closed unless a need is approved.
Least privilege
Open the smallest door, only for who needs it, when needed.
Accountability
Log who crossed, when, and why.

Note: Saying “62443” alone doesn’t explain anything it’s a series of standards. We reference only the parts about zones & conduits.

Controls & Rules (common in DPI/DPT)
  • Move one level at a time; no elevator skipping floors.
  • Default‑deny between major levels; open only documented approved paths.
  • Use a DMZ (L3.5) to broker operations ↔ enterprise/cloud flows.
  • Admin/vendor access via a jump‑host with MFA and audit logging.
  • Inside a zone, fine‑grained micro‑cuts are generally not enforced by policy.
  • Only IT‑managed services belong in the DMZ; control‑impacting apps sit in operations levels.
DPI / DPT Variants (descriptive)
Essential
  • Where considered: small sites / early stage
  • Components: layered LAN + NGFW; L3.5 DMZ; most services in Regional DC
  • Use cases: few zones; basic resilience; central teams own services
Premium
  • Where considered: mid‑size sites needing local speed/resilience
  • Components: adds on‑site services (file share on infrae2, AD/DNS/NTP/DHCP, backup target)
  • Use cases: more zones, heavier data movement, partial autonomy
Platinum
  • Where considered: large/regulated/24×7 or WAN‑sensitive sites
  • Components: full on‑site stack (compute & storage), AD/DNS/DHCP (2x), backup on‑site + replicated, Citrix jump; optional IDS/NAB; optional DB engines at L3.5
  • Use cases: highest autonomy and resilience; must continue if WAN is down
